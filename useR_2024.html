opentimeseries: An R Package to Transform (Ugly) Data Publications into Machine-Readable Time Series

Because publications by public data providers focus on a broader audience, their datasets are often not convenient to use for research. To mitigate this problem, the opentimeseries R package provides the time series and official statistics communities with reusable code to conveniently source data from public sources. By splitting data and metadata into two different files, a long format CSV file for the data and a JSON file for multi-lingual metainformation, the package generates output that is inclusive to humans (and their favorite spreadsheet software) and convenient to ingest for machines. This data output is the starting point not only for intertemporal comparisons but also for versioning of time series, as it is needed for real-time analysis or evaluation of forecasts. The package open-sources a data ingestion framework, proven through its longtime usage in monitoring the Swiss economy at the KOF Swiss Economic Institute at ETH Zurich, for the first time. We explicitly chose the R ecosystem with its great documentation and boiler plating tools to encourage dataset maintenance and community contributions across different fields that use public data for research.

Gains for the attendee

  • learn about a reusable approach that is transferable to other countries and fields
  • exchange about the timelines/dynamics of data pipeline automation efforts in the public sector and what the research community can do in the meantime
  • learn about a strategy to continously adapt to modernization of sources (migration to APIs)
  • discuss efficient efficient versioning of revised time series and tradeoffs

Open Time Series Initiative

The Open Time Series Initiative aims to bridge the gap between academic research and official statistics. Founded and developed at the Research Software Engineering and Economic Data section at KOF Lab at ETH Zurich (RSEED), the initiative plans to start its activities around publicly macroeconomic data. First, we publish an R based framework to provide the time series community with reusable code to source publicly available data and process data publications into time series. Second, we plan to publish maintain and version over 100’000 machine-readable macroeconomic time series following modern open science standards (FAIR principles). Third, we integrate processing of publicly available data into academic teaching. Our explaroatory discussion with the Swiss Federal Statistical Office (FSO) and the pionieering collaboration with the Swiss Secretariat of Economic affairs (SECO) make us optimistic that our starting push will lead to spillovers to other fields of research and parts of official statistics.

  • publish framework
  • publish 100K macro economic time series based on publicly available data, following fair principles
  • integrates publicly available data into academic teaching
  • pilots and pioneering efforts gives a hope to create a push big enough to cause spill overs to other research communities.

The technical implementation of the open sourced initiative is backed by an R package, and integrates this framework and public data into teaching and community events.

Developed by the Competence Hub for Research Software Engineering and Economic Data (RSEED) at the KOF Lab at ETH Zurich, in collaboration with the Swiss Federal Statistical Office (FSO) and with the pioneering efforts of the State Secretariat for Economic Affairs (SECO), the initiative aims not only to encourage public data providers to make their data FAIRer (findable, accessible, interoperable and reusable), but also to train the next generation of public servants to become Open Science leaders who are familiar with the Swiss data landscape.

Here a link to the Open Time Series funding proposal: https://rseed.ch/opnts-ord-proposal/

Learnings:

  • learn about how to make data publications inclusive
  • how to make public data FAIR (findable, accessible, interoperable reusable), - discuss why many open data approaches stop shortly before being truly useful for research
